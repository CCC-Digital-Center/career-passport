## **1. Introduction**

**[Administrative and Legal Notice]**

Notice is hereby given that the Foothill-De Anza Community College District, located in Los Altos Hills, California ("District"), hereby invites qualified firms ("Proposers") to submit proposals in response to this Request for Proposals (RFP) for the California Career Passport Platform. The District is seeking experienced vendors to collaborate on the design, development, and operation of a learner-controlled digital credential wallet that enables Californians to securely store, manage, and share verified records of their skills, academic achievements, and workforce experience.

This RFP is being issued by the District on behalf of and in support of the California Community Colleges Chancellor’s Office (“CCCCO”) in its role as the administering agency for the California Career Passport program. The District is serving in its role as the operational lead and fiscal agent of the California Community Colleges Digital Center for Innovation, Transformation, and Equity (“Digital Center”), a joint initiative of the District and the CCCCO established to support shared digital services and technology-enabled solutions that benefit the CCC system as a whole. Through this collaborative structure, the CCCCO provides statewide leadership and strategic direction, with the District supporting the operational execution of the procurement process. Proposers are expected to respond with an understanding that the solution(s) selected through this RFP are intended to operate at statewide scale and in alignment with CCC systemwide priorities. The Career Passport is being developed in statutory collaboration with the California Labor and Workforce Development Agency and the Office of Cradle-to-Career Data, as required by the California Higher Education Trailer Bill (Committee on Budget, AB 123, Chapter 9, Statutes of 2025).

 

This Career Passport procurement is structured as a three-phase process: 

Phase 1 — Innovation Concept Narrative and Pilot Readiness: Proposers submit an Innovation Concept Narrative (**maximum 20 pages**) and a Pilot Readiness Package. The District will select approximately five Proposers to participate in the pilot phase, with between $125,000 to $150,000 granted to each selected Proposer to support pilot execution.

Phase 2 — Pilot Demonstration: Selected Proposers will implement a live pilot demonstration over a period of approximately ten weeks, testing both technical interoperability and usability with real California learners from the populations this program is designed to serve. At the conclusion of Phase 2, the District will evaluate pilot outcomes and invite top performers to submit production proposals.

Phase 3 — Production Proposal: Proposers invited to Phase 3 will submit a production-ready proposal informed by pilot findings. The District intends to award a final contract to a qualified Proposer for a term of up to five years. Selection for Phase 2 does not guarantee invitation to Phase 3. The District reserves the right to modify scope, negotiate terms, or discontinue the procurement at any phase.

The proposals must address all required components. Submissions that address only a portion of the scope (e.g., limited to a single service area) may be considered non-responsive and not evaluated further. The selected firm(s) will be responsible for delivering high-quality, innovative, and sustainable solutions that support the statewide objectives of this initiative for a term of up to five years.

 

Proposers shall demonstrate a proven record of performance and sufficient capacity to provide the services described in Section 5 – Scope of Work. Each Proposer must possess adequate and readily available resources, including qualified personnel, technical expertise, financial stability, and administrative support, necessary to perform the services in a competent, timely, and uninterrupted manner, consistent with the standards and expectations set forth in this RFP.

**[5 PROJECT SCOPE OF WORK]**

**Introduction**

The California Career Passport is a response to Governor Newsom's Master Plan for Career Education, which calls for a mechanism that enables people to attain well-paid work by ensuring that skills built through apprenticeships, industry credentials, military service, noncredit programs, and work experience can be recognized by employers and colleges alike. The Career Passport will deliver on that vision by providing Californians with a secure, learner-controlled digital tool that displays their verifiable academic records, licenses, industry credentials, badges, and military service. This will give employers a fuller picture of what Californians know and can do, and open opportunity to workers whose skills and experience extend beyond any single credential.

<span style="text-decoration:underline;">Skills-Informed Hiring in California</span>

California's approach to education and workforce development has long reflected a belief that talent takes many forms. Beginning in 2017, the California Mapping Articulated Pathways (MAP) Initiative helps students across all 116 California Community Colleges earn college credit for prior learning (CPL) from work experience, industry certifications, and military training, enabling faster, lower cost degree completion by converting existing skills into academic credit. The California Department of Human Resources has expanded pathways to state employment across more than 170 job classifications, recognizing that skills developed through apprenticeships, military service, industry training, noncredit programs, and work experience enrich the talent available to state employers and should not be invisible to hiring managers. This reflects a broader national shift toward skills-informed talent practices, as employers across sectors increasingly seek to understand what candidates can do alongside the credentials they hold.

The Career Passport is California's infrastructure investment in that shift: a tool that makes verified skills visible, portable, and actionable for the Californians who have built them.

<span style="text-decoration:underline;">California’s Learners and Their Credentials</span>

California has the most diverse postsecondary student body in the nation. Its students are recent high school graduates and returning adults, veterans and working adults, people new to the country and those whose families have been here for generations, individuals moving between careers and those entering the workforce for the first time. California's education systems are designed to support this movement: between community colleges, through transfer pathways to California State Universities and University of California campuses, and across courses available in person and online through the California Virtual College network. As a result, the credentials that document these experiences are extraordinarily varied, including academic transcripts, industry certifications, licenses issued by professional boards, badges earned through workforce programs, and records of skills developed through experience. 

The Career Passport is designed to be used by all Californians, to hold all of their credentials, a living, learner-controlled portfolio that grows with them and provides them value across a lifetime of learning and work. 

<span style="text-decoration:underline;">Increasing Trust for Employers</span>

Employers need infrastructure that makes verification fast, reliable, and scalable. Verifying that a candidate's skills and credentials are authentic requires third-party services, manual processes, and significant administrative overhead for both the employer and the applicant alike. 

For California employers committed to skills-informed talent practices, the Career Passport is infrastructure that makes those practices operationally viable at scale. The W3C Verifiable Credentials that jobseekers can hold in the Career Passport, including the modernized California eTranscript currently in development, carry cryptographic proof of their authenticity so that employers can verify instantly that a credential was issued by a known and authorized source, has not been tampered with, and belongs to the person presenting it, all without contacting the issuing institution, without a third-party verification service, and without delay. 

As the ecosystem matures, this verification capability can be embedded directly into the applicant tracking systems, HR platforms, and hiring workflows that employers already use, making skills-informed hiring not just possible, but frictionless.

<span style="text-decoration:underline;">Private by Design, Universally Accessible, and Genuinely Trustworthy</span>

Many Californians who stand to benefit most from the Career Passport have compelling reasons to be cautious about who sees their personal information. A formerly incarcerated person applying for a job. An undocumented student seeking credit for prior learning. A veteran navigating a new career. Each of these individuals has reason to want control over what they share and with whom. 

The Career Passport is built on a foundational commitment: the credentials earned by Californians belong to them, and the digital system that holds those credentials works in their interest — not the interest of the wallet vendor, the credential issuer, or the verifier. Credential holders control what they share, with whom, and for what purpose. When a Californian presents a credential, only the information necessary for that specific interaction is shared. The system does not create records of where credentials have been presented or what information was disclosed, without the explicit knowledge and control of the holder.

The Career Passport will also work for Californians whose access to technology is limited or shared. A wallet that functions only on the latest smartphone with reliable broadband is not a wallet for all Californians. The system will be designed for people who share devices, rely on intermittent connectivity, or access the internet primarily through a phone, and it must remain accessible even when their technology circumstances change.

Looking ahead, the open standards-based foundation of the Career Passport creates the conditions for greater flexibility in how and where Californians store their credentials, including approaches that give individuals direct control over their own data infrastructure, such as wallet-attached storage. Future iterations may also support multilingual credential display, offline access for holders without reliable connectivity, and seamless integration with employer and institutional systems that make credential verification a natural part of existing workflows rather than a separate step.

For now, the priority is a system that is private by design, universally accessible, and genuinely trustworthy for every Californian who uses it.

This procurement is one of two concurrent Request for Proposals processes through which the Chancellor's Office (via the Digital Center) is building the technical infrastructure of the Career Passport. The first RFP addresses the modernization of eTranscript California, the statewide academic transcript exchange network. This RFP addresses the Career Passport itself: the learner-controlled wallet through which learners and jobseekers in California will access, manage, and share their credentials. The two procurements are designed to be interoperable, and vendors responding to this RFP should understand how their proposed solution relates to the eTranscript CA modernization effort.

## **2. Section One — Innovation Concept Narrative**

Phase One is intended to surface strategic approaches for a learner-controlled credential wallet that serves all Californians, particularly those who have been systematically excluded from credential-based opportunity. The CCCCO seeks solutions that demonstrate architectural coherence, equity-centered design, interoperability grounded in open standards, and long-term sustainability as public infrastructure.

### **Question 1 — A Vision of Equity**

*(Suggested length: approximately 3 pages)*

*California's Career Passport is an infrastructure investment in economic mobility for all Californians, including those who have been systematically excluded from opportunity. This includes people whose skills, experience, and credentials have not been visible to employers in ways that lead to good jobs. Describe your understanding of this challenge and how your proposed approach addresses it as an equity infrastructure problem, not merely a credential management one.*

**In your response, address:**



* The structural barriers that prevent Californians from having their skills and credentials recognized in hiring and advancement decisions. Focus particularly on those without bachelor's degrees, from low-income backgrounds, from immigrant and undocumented communities, from the military, and from the formerly incarcerated populations.
* How your approach centers the needs of the populations described above as design requirements, not as edge cases or accessibility add-ons. Describe a specific scenario in which a Californian from one of these populations uses your wallet to access an opportunity they could not have accessed otherwise, and where your design choices made the difference.
* How the Career Passport wallet fits within California's shift from degree-based to skills-informed hiring, and how it serves learners across academic and non-academic pathways including apprenticeships, industry credentials, military service, and noncredit programs.
* How your approach supports wallet interoperability, giving the ability for a Californian who already has credentials in another wallet to aggregate and present those credentials alongside Career Passport-issued credentials, without requiring them to start over or abandon existing records.
* What success looks like at the end of the pilot period, and what it looks like at statewide scale. Describe how you would know the Career Passport is working for the people it was designed to serve, not just technically, but in terms of real outcomes for real Californians.


### **Question 2 — Technical Architecture and Open Standards**

*(Suggested length: approximately 3 pages)*

*The Career Passport wallet must be accessible by any Californian regardless of device or platform. Foundational development should be based on community-developed, open standards. Describe the technical architecture of your proposed wallet and how it addresses the following requirements.*

**In your response, address:**



* How your wallet ensures that credential content and cryptographic keys remain under the control of the holder not the vendor. Describe whether your architecture is custodial, non-custodial, or a hybrid approach, and explain what access, if any, your organization has to a holder's credential content or keys. Describe how a holder's access to their credentials is maintained if your organization ceases to operate or loses the contract.
* Your proposed approach to credential exchange: the protocol or protocols your wallet implements for receiving and presenting credentials. Whichever protocol you implement, describe your rationale for that choice, how your approach relates to other widely adopted exchange protocols for education and workforce credentials, and how interoperability is maintained across them. Proposals implementing only proprietary exchange protocols will not be considered.
* How your wallet balances privacy, security, and accountability during credential presentation and verification. In particular, address whether your architecture can support verification without remote dependencies or metadata leakage to issuing authorities, especially as the system scales. Describe how your chosen exchange protocol addresses the privacy properties required by this RFP, specifically selective disclosure and unlinkability, and how your architecture prevents metadata leakage during both issuance and presentation flows. Where your protocol choice involves tradeoffs between these properties, describe how your design mitigates them. Separately, describe how your wallet handles logging of credential presentation events, including whether any such data is transmitted to vendor servers, who can access it, and how the holder can control or delete it.
* How your wallet gives holders meaningful control over what they share. Describe how your wallet makes selective disclosure comprehensible and actionable for holders with varying levels of technical literacy, not just technically possible. Users should be able to understand what credential claims are being requested, choose which claims to share, and complete a presentation without disclosing information beyond what the interaction requires. 
* How your approach to credential display is designed to evolve as standards mature. Describe how your implementation uses issuer-provided metadata to render credentials, and how your architecture allows display rendering to be updated without requiring redesign of your core credential handling.


### **Question 3 — A Wallet for All Credentials**

*(Suggested length: approximately 3 pages)*

*The Career Passport will hold credentials issued by a wide range of organizations, including community colleges, universities, workforce training providers, military branches, industry certification bodies, apprenticeship programs, and licensing boards. While the State's issuance infrastructure establishes a shared technical foundation based on W3C Verifiable Credentials 2.0 (VCDM) and Open Badges v3.0 (OBv3), the credentials that flow through it will vary enormously in their metadata richness, their skills documentation maturity, and the complexity of the learning and employment experiences they represent. Describe how your wallet serves as a reliable, consistent holder for this full range of credentials and the people who earn them.*

**In your response, address:**



* How your wallet receives and displays W3C VCDM-conformant credentials whose metadata richness varies significantly, from credentials with fully structured, machine-readable skills data to credentials that contain only basic issuance information. Describe what the holder experience looks like across this spectrum, and how your display approach remains meaningful and useful even when credential metadata is sparse.
* How your wallet accommodates credentials from issuers with widely varying levels of technical sophistication and metadata completeness, from large institutions with dedicated technical infrastructure to small workforce providers and licensing boards with minimal capacity. Describe how your architecture handles credentials that meet the W3C VCDM standard regardless of the issuance pipeline they came through, and how the holder experience remains consistent even when credential metadata is sparse or incomplete.
* How your wallet handles a credential it does not recognize, such as an unfamiliar issuer, an unfamiliar credential type, or a credential whose display properties are absent or incomplete. Describe how your wallet degrades gracefully in these cases and what the holder experience looks like when the wallet encounters something it was not explicitly designed for.
* How you have tested or would demonstrate multi-issuer, multi-credential interoperability within a shared standards framework. Describe your experience with interoperability testing across credential types, issuers, and exchange protocols, including testing with applications and verifiers that implement protocols different from your own.
* How your wallet manages the credential lifecycle, including credentials that are revoked, superseded, or issued by organizations that evolve their technical infrastructure. Describe how your architecture ensures that a holder's credentials remain accessible and accurately represented as the issuer ecosystem around them changes and how a holder can take their credentials with them if they move to a different wallet or if your organization no longer provides this service.


### **Question 4 — Integration with California's Education and Workforce Ecosystem**

*(Suggested length: approximately 3 pages)*

*The Career Passport is one component of a broader statewide infrastructure that includes academic record systems, credit for prior learning pathways, workforce data systems, employer hiring tools, and emerging state digital identity infrastructure. Describe how your proposed wallet integrates with and strengthens this ecosystem.*

**In your response, address:**



* Describe your experience or approach to supporting credential presentation across the range of systems that Californians interact with, including academic institutions, employer applicant tracking systems, government HR platforms, workforce agencies, and licensing boards. Where you have deployed or tested integrations with verifier systems of varying technical sophistication, describe what you learned. Where you have not yet deployed at scale, describe your architectural approach and how it has been validated. In either case, describe how your wallet ensures that any conformant verifier can receive and interpret a credential presentation without requiring bespoke integration, a prior vendor relationship, or proprietary tooling.
* How your wallet relates to California's emerging digital identity infrastructure. California is developing interoperable digital identity capabilities across multiple state systems, the CA DMV mobile driver's license (mDL) initiative being one active example. Describe how your technical architecture aligns with or complements existing state digital identity efforts, and how your approach supports a Californian who may already have a state-issued digital identity credential as part of their user experience.
* How your proposed approach strengthens California's broader shift toward skills-informed hiring, not merely as a technical integration but as a practical tool that employers of varying sizes and technical sophistication can actually use. Describe how your wallet makes it easier for a California employer to act on credential information, and what specific design choices you have made to support employer adoption at scale.
* How you think about the Career Passport wallet's role in a future where California's credential ecosystem is more mature: more issuers, more verifiers, more credential types, more interoperable options. ### **Question 5 — Governance, Trust, and Long-term Stewardship**

*(Suggested length: approximately 3 pages)*

*The Career Passport is public infrastructure. It will hold credentials that Californians depend on for employment, advancement, and opportunity. It must remain trustworthy, accessible, and under learner control regardless of what happens in the vendor market, the standards landscape, or the State's procurement cycles. Describe how your proposed approach is designed for long-term stewardship, not just initial delivery.*

**In your response, address:**



* How a Californian's credentials remain accessible and verifiable if your organization is acquired, restructured, or no longer holds the State contract. Describe the technical and contractual mechanisms that ensure credential portability and continuity of access independent of any single vendor relationship.
* How your wallet is designed so that any conformant verifier can receive a credential presentation without requiring a prior relationship with your organization, executing a memorandum of understanding, or using proprietary tooling. Describe how your business model and technical architecture support an open verifier ecosystem rather than a closed one. Where you have deployed or tested credential exchange with verifier systems, describe what you learned. Where you have not yet deployed or tested, describe your architectural approach and how it has been validated.
* How you approach the evolution of open standards over the life of the contract. The credential wallet standards landscape, including exchange protocols, display rendering specifications, and cryptographic schemes, is actively developing. Describe how your organization stays current with and contributes to this evolution, and how your architecture accommodates standards changes without requiring disruptive redesign or contract renegotiation.
* How you govern changes to the wallet over time, including updates to credential display, exchange protocols, identity model, and user experience. Describe what community input, user feedback, and State oversight look like in your governance model, and how you balance the need for innovation with the stability that public infrastructure requires.
* How you manage software changes, updates, and service interruptions in ways that protect users who depend on the wallet for time-sensitive activities. Describe how your release and maintenance practices account for users with limited technical support, shared or low-end devices, and varying levels of digital literacy, including how you communicate changes in plain language, how you maintain continuity of access during transitions, and how you incorporate ongoing accessibility requests, language access needs, and usability feedback from diverse user communities into your product roadmap over time.
* How you approach security, incident response, and the protection of holder data at statewide scale. Describe your approach to identifying and responding to security incidents, how you communicate with affected holders, and what your architecture does to limit the blast radius of a breach or compromise.

### **Pilot Design Overview**

The pilot serves two equally weighted purposes: demonstrating technical interoperability and demonstrating usability with real California learners from the populations this program is designed to serve.  

**Cohort Structure**

A cohort of approximately 50 participants will engage with each selected Proposer solution. These participants will be California students, workers, or jobseekers recruited from a variety of California summer programs, bootcamps, or workforce activities. All participants within each cohort will utilize a single Proposer solution. The District will assign cohorts to Proposers following pilot selection. Cohort sizes and program contexts will be confirmed in the pilot design addendum issued with Phase 2 selection notices.

**Unassisted Protocol**

The pilot cohort activity will be conducted without Proposer staff present. Participants will complete the wallet workflow independently, supported only by the participant guide and by facilitators providing process support only. This reflects the real-world conditions under which the Career Passport will operate at scale.

**Pre-Pilot Integration Check**

Within 30 days of selection, each Proposer must pass a technical integration check demonstrating that their wallet can receive a credential issued by the State test issuer and display it correctly without vendor-side intervention. Failure to pass the integration check will result in contract termination at 0% payment. One remediation window will be provided.

**Credential Diversity Requirement**

Each Proposer must demonstrate during the pilot that their wallet holds a minimum of 3 credential types from at least 2 issuers, including at minimum the State-provided test issuer. Credential types must span at least 2 categories: academic, workforce, badge, or other W3C VCDM-conformant credential type. Additional details will be provided in the pilot design addendum issued with Phase 2 selection notices.

**Verification**

The interoperability demonstration is not a controlled test. Verification will occur as part of the live cohort activity, in which participants share credentials with a program-side verifier. Wallet functionality that cannot be verified through this live exchange will not satisfy the interoperability requirement. Additional details on the program-side verifier will be provided in the pilot design addendum issued with Phase 2 selection notices.

**User Research Instruments**

Each cohort activity will use two instruments: a paper-based task completion log administered during the activity, and a short experience debrief completed by participants immediately afterward. Vendors will receive anonymized results and must submit a structured user research analysis as part of their pilot deliverables.


### **Pilot Criteria**

**Tier 1 — Interoperability (hard pass/fail)**

The credential issued by the State test issuer must verify successfully using the District-provided verifier tool during the live cohort activity without vendor-side intervention. Proposers who fail Tier 1 during the cohort activity may request one remediation demonstration within 5 business days. Failure to pass remediation disqualifies the Proposer from Phase 3.

**Tier 2 — Usability (threshold plus analysis)**

A significant portion of cohort participants must complete the full workflow independently (wallet access, credential receipt, credential presentation, and verification) without facilitator technical assistance. Proposers are not automatically disqualified if a significant portion of cohort participants do not complete the workflow, but must demonstrate in their Phase 3 production proposal what they would do differently and why. How a Proposer responds to and learns from pilot challenges is itself an evaluation criterion for Phase 3.

**Tier 2b — Accessibility Observation **

The District will conduct a structured accessibility observation during each pilot using a consistent methodology across all vendor cohorts. Findings will be documented and provided to vendors as part of their pilot debrief. Vendors must address accessibility findings directly in their Phase 3 production proposal. The District will weight accessibility responsiveness as a scored criterion in Phase 3 evaluation.
